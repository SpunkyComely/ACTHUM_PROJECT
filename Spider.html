Spider

Spiders are arachnids with eight legs and two body segments. They are known for spinning silk webs to catch prey, though not all spiders build webs. Most spiders are carnivorous and feed on insects, playing a key role in controlling pest populations. Spiders can be found in almost every environment, from forests to deserts to homes. Despite common fears, most spiders are harmless to humans.

Habitat

Spiders can live in a wide variety of habitats, including forests, deserts, grasslands, caves, and even underwater. Many spiders build webs in trees, bushes, or corners of buildings to catch prey. Some, like tarantulas, dig burrows or live under rocks and logs. Spiders are highly adaptable and can be found on every continent except Antarctica. They often choose habitats that provide shelter, food, and the right humidity for survival.

Diet

Spiders are carnivorous and primarily feed on insects such as flies, mosquitoes, and moths. They catch their prey using webs, ambush tactics, or active hunting, depending on the species. Once caught, spiders inject venom to immobilize the prey and then use digestive enzymes to liquefy it for consumption. Some larger spider species can eat small birds, lizards, or rodents. Spiders play a crucial role in natural pest control by keeping insect populations in check.

Social Behavior

Most spiders are solitary creatures, spending their lives alone except during mating. They often defend their webs or territories from other spiders. Some species, like social spiders, do live in groups and cooperate in web building and hunting. Even in these social species, cannibalism can occur, especially if food is scarce. Overall, spider social behavior is limited, with most interactions driven by survival or reproduction.
