Iguana

Iguanas are large, herbivorous lizards native to Central and South America, with some species also found in the Caribbean. They are known for their long tails, sharp claws, and distinctive crests along their backs and heads. Iguanas are primarily plant-eaters, feeding on leaves, flowers, fruits, and vegetables, although they may occasionally eat insects. They are excellent climbers and spend much of their time in trees, where they seek safety and warmth from the sun. Iguanas are cold-blooded reptiles, meaning they rely on external heat sources to regulate their body temperature.

Habitat

Iguanas are primarily found in tropical and subtropical regions of Central and South America, as well as some Caribbean islands. They thrive in warm environments, often inhabiting forests, rainforests, and coastal areas, where they have access to both food and shelter. Many iguana species prefer areas with abundant vegetation, as their herbivorous diet relies on leaves, fruits, and flowers. They are also commonly found in rocky hillsides or along riverbanks, where they can easily climb trees or bask in the sun. Iguanas are arboreal, meaning they spend a significant amount of time in trees, but they also seek refuge in burrows or crevices to hide from predators. Coastal iguanas, such as the marine iguana, are adapted to life near the sea and can even swim in the ocean to forage for food.

Diet

Iguanas are herbivores, primarily feeding on a variety of plant material such as leaves, flowers, fruits, and vegetables. Their diet consists mostly of leafy greens like collard greens, dandelion greens, and hibiscus leaves, which provide essential nutrients. They also consume fruits like mangoes, bananas, and papayas, along with some types of flowers, including jasmine and orchids. While iguanas mainly eat plant matter, they occasionally nibble on tender plant stems or vines. Because they are cold-blooded, iguanas rely on a nutrient-rich diet to maintain their energy and body function.

Social Behavior

Iguanas are generally solitary animals, especially when it comes to adults, who prefer to live alone and establish their own territories. They are most social during mating season, when males may compete for the attention of females, often engaging in displays of dominance. Male iguanas may puff out their throats, bob their heads, or make hissing sounds to assert their dominance or warn other males away. Juvenile iguanas, on the other hand, may tolerate living in loose groups, especially in areas with abundant food and shelter. While they are not highly social, iguanas do communicate with one another through body language, such as tail flicking or changes in posture.
